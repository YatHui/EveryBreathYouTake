Health and Air pollution

In the graphs below you can compare deaths and diseases attributed to air pollution between 2010 to 2019. All data is gender and age-standardized and further explainations about the data can be found below the graph.

Death from household air pollution from solid fuels (per 100.000 people) Indoor air pollution caused by burning solid fuel sources – such as firewood, crop waste and dung – for cooking or heating. Burning of such fuel sources in poor households with poor air circulation is, according to WHO, the world’s largest single environmental health risk.
Death from ambient particulate matter pollution(per 100.000 people) Particular matter (PM) is everything in the air that is not a gas. Those with a diameter of 2.5 micrometers or less can penetrate the lung barrier and enter a person’s blood system and are considered the most health-damaging.
Death from all causes attributed to air pollution (per 100.000 people) Considers all possible causes of death that can be linked tor air pollution such as pre-exiting respiratory conditions.
Death from ambient ozone pollution (per 100.000 people) The impact from ground-level ozone pollution is less than those from solid fuel sources and PM 2.5 and is therefor not as researched but the data still shows considerable deaths.

DALY stands for Disability-Adjusted Life Year, and it is a measure used in public health to assess the overall burden of diseases, injuries,
and other health conditions on a population. It uses years of life lost, and years lived with disability to quantify the impact of health issues. The data shows the DALY calculated on the different types of pollution.

The PM 2.5 graphs are color-coded based on the the safe-levels going from green (safe) to red (unsafe)
