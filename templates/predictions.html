Air Quality and Goals

Nitrogen dioxide and air particulate matters (pm) are the common indicators to measure air pollution. Two sizes of particulate matter are measured - coarse particles bigger than 10 microns (pm10) and fine particulates smaller than 2.5 microns (pm2.5). Our bodies can filter out the coarser particles, such as by trapping them with the hair in our nose, or we can cough them out. However, fine particulates are so small that when we breathe them in, they can easily get into our lungs and bloodstreams. This is why they cause respiratory and heart diesases.

This is also why we are looking at pm2.5 datasets in this data project. The datasets comes from the World Heath Organisation’s Air Quality Database.

“99% of the world’s population live in a place where the air pollution level is above WHO guidelines.” World Health Organisation

Predictions
Since the WHO datasets only go up to 2019, a linear regression model is used to predict levels up to 2023. This assumes business as usual, for example, no global pandemic or wars taking place. Predictions up to 2023 are shown with a dotted line.



Air Quality Guidance
In 2005, the World Health Organisation issued the first Air Quality Guidance (AQG). This shows the level of air pollutants is considered safe from causing any air pollution related diseases and deaths. The AQG has been updated in 2018 and 2021 and shown on the plot with a dash-dot line. The levels are:


  • 2005: 10 µg/m³ per year
  • 2018: 5.5 µg/m³ per year
  • 2021: 5.0 µg/m³ per year




Which country is on track to reaching it? Even if some countries may seem far from reaching the target levels, bear in mind that this is the ideal target where there is no risk of getting ill by air pollution.
